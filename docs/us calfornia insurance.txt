What insurance is and why it matters
Insurance is a financial tool that lets many people pool money to protect themselves against
big, unpredictable losses (like a house fire, car crash, illness, or death of a breadwinner).The
basic idea is that you pay a relatively small, predictable amount called a premium to an insurance
company, and in return the company promises to pay if certain bad events (risks) actually
happen.
Why this is important:
It protects individuals and businesses from financial ruin after major losses.
It makes many activities possible or cheaper (e.g., driving a car, taking a mortgage, running a
business)because lenders and partners know losses are covered.
It creates a more stable economy by spreading risk widely instead of letting a few unlucky
people bear huge costs alone.
Key terms you’ll see repeatedly:
Policy – the legal contract between you and the insurer.
Premium – what you pay (monthly, yearly, etc.).
Claim – a request for payment when a covered event happens.
Deductible – the amount you pay out-of-pocket before insurance pays (common in
property/health).
Benefit – the money or service the insurer provides (e.g., a death benefit in life insurance).
How the U.S. insurance system works (high level)
State‑based regulation with national coordination
In the United States, insurance is mainly regulated by each state, not by the federal
government.This state‑based system is reinforced by a federal law called the McCarran–
Ferguson Act, which says that states are the primary regulators of the “business of
insurance.”
[1] [2]
Every state has an insurance department (or similar agency) and an insurance commissioner or
director to oversee insurance companies’ solvency (ability to pay claims), rates, and market
conduct (how they sell and service policies).The National Association of Insurance
Commissioners (NAIC)is a standard‑setting body formed by the state regulators;it develops
model laws, standards, and best practices and coordinates supervision across all 50states and
U.S.territories.
[3] [4] [2]
Federal agencies still touch insurance in limited ways—for example, financial stability oversight
and certain consumer‑protection or securities rules—but day‑to‑day insurance regulation is
largely state‑driven.
[2] [1]
Across the U.S., most insurance is provided by private insurance companies that:
Design products and file them with state regulators when required.
Collect premiums.
Invest those premiums (the “float”)under strict solvency rules.
Pay covered claims according to the policy and state law.
States also have “guaranty associations” that give limited protection to policyholders if a
licensed insurer becomes insolvent (fails financially).In California, for example, life insurance
and annuity customers may be partially protected by the California Life and Health Insurance
Guarantee Association (CLHIGA)if the insurer was licensed in California.

Policyholder (or contract owner):The person or business that owns the policy, pays
premiums, and has contractual rights (e.g., to change beneficiaries or cancel the policy).
Beneficiary:The person or entity that receives benefits (for example, the person who
receives life insurance proceeds when the insured dies).

Key participants in the U.S. insurance ecosystem
Policyholders and beneficiaries
Insurance companies (insurers)
Insurers underwrite risk (decide what to insure, at what price) and promise to pay covered claims
in exchange for premiums.They must be licensed in each state where they do business and are
subject to capital, reserve, and reporting requirements so regulators can monitor solvency and
fair practices.
[4] [2]
Reinsurers
Reinsurers are “insurance for insurance companies.” Aprimary insurer can transfer part of the
risk it has taken on to a reinsurer in exchange for a portion of the premium, helping the primary
insurer manage large or concentrated exposures (e.g., hurricanes, earthquakes).Reinsurers are
regulated in ways similar to insurers but often work mostly behind the scenes.
[2]
Agents and brokers
Agents:Typically represent one or more insurance companies and sell their products.They
must be licensed by the state insurance department where they operate.
[6] [7]
Brokers:Represent the buyer (policyholder) rather than a single insurer and help shop
multiple insurers.They also must be licensed, and their compensation and duties are
regulated by state law.
[4]
For specialized products like variable life insurance or variable annuities (which are treated as
securities), sellers usuallyneed both a state insurance license and registration with FINRA(the
securities self‑regulatory body), and must provide a prospectus describing investment options.
[8]
Regulators
State insurance departments (e.g., California Department of Insurance)license companies
and producers, review rates and policy forms, oversee solvency, enforce market‑conduct
rules, and handle consumer complaints.
[3] [6] [4]
NAICcoordinates among state regulators with model laws, accreditation programs, and
shared tools and data.
[3] [2]
Overall structure and functioning of the U.S. insurance industry
At a high level, the U.S.insurance industry is:
Decentralized but coordinated: Each state has its own insurance code and regulator, but
NAICand various model laws create a high degree of uniformity.
[4] [2] [3]
Segmented by line: Companies may specialize in life/annuity, property & casualty, health, or
be diversified groups with different licensed entities for each line.
Distribution‑driven:Products reach consumers through agents, brokers,
direct‑to‑consumer websites, employer benefit programs, banks, and financial advisors.
Heavily capital‑regulated: State regulators require detailed financial reporting and
minimum capital and reserve levels so insurers can pay claims even in stress scenarios.
[2] [4]
Consumer‑protectionoriented: Regulators review rates, policy language, marketing
practices, claims handling, and replacement transactions;they also operate consumer
hotlines and complaint processes.

California’s insurance system: the basics
The California Department of Insurance (CDI)
California has one of the largest and most active insurance markets in the world, and the
California Department of Insurance (CDI)is its main regulator.
[10] [6]
Key points:
CDI is led by an elected Insurance Commissioner, meaning the regulator is directly
accountable to voters as well as to the law.
[11] [6]
CDI regulates more than 1,600insurance companies and licenses over 500,000agents,
brokers, adjusters, and other insurance professionals.
[10] [6]
Its core mission is consumer protection:ensuring fair rates, solvency of insurers, honest
sales practices, and fair claims handling.
[6] [9] [10]
CDI is often described as California’s largest consumer‑protection agency and oversees the vast
majority of the state’s insurance industry.
[12] [6]
How California funds and structures regulation
CDI is primarily funded by license fees, assessments on insurers, and special fees such as
Proposition 103 recoupment fees, not by the state’s general tax revenues.It employs roughly
1,300–1,400staff working across licensing, enforcement, legal, consumer services, and fraud
investigation units.
[11] [10] [6]
How insurance operates in California
Licensing and market entry
Any insurer that wants to sell insurance in California typically must:
Be licensed by CDI for each line of insurance (life, property, etc.).
Maintain required capital and reserves.
File certain policy forms and, for many lines, file rates or ask for rate approval.
[1] [6]
CDIalso licenses agents and brokers, setting standards for training, exams, and continuing
education.
[13] [6]
Rate and form regulation; Proposition 103
California is known for strong rate regulation, especially in personal‑lines property and
casualty (auto, homeowners, etc.).Proposition 103, a voter‑approved law, requires prior approval
of certain insurance rates and increased transparency in rate filings.CDI reviews whether
proposed rates are excessive, inadequate, or unfairly discriminatory before allowing them.
[11] [6]
Consumer protection and enforcement
CDI:
Investigates consumer complaints and recovers tens of millions of dollars a year for
policyholders through complaint resolution.
[9] [10]
Investigates and prosecutes insurance fraud.
Conducts “market conduct” exams of insurers to check sales practices, underwriting, and
claims handling.
[6]
Oversees solvency examinations to ensure companies can pay claims.

Life insurance in California: what it is
Life insurance is a contract where an insurer promises to pay a lump sum (the death benefit) to
named beneficiaries when the insured person dies, in exchange for premiums paid during the
insured’s lifetime.California’s consumer guides treat life insurance as a key tool for financial
planning, especially to protect dependents or manage estate and business needs.
[8] [7]
In California, life insurance products are sold by insurers that are licensed by CDI, and owners of
life policies from such licensed companies may receive partial protection through CLHIGAif the
insurer fails financially.CDIprovides free, official consumer guides to help people understand
life insurance and make informed choices.
[7] [5] [8]
How life insurance works in California
Mechanically, life insurance in California works like elsewhere in the U.S., but under
California‑specific rules:
1.Applicationand underwriting
You apply through an agent, broker, or directly with an insurer.
The insurer evaluates your risk (age, health, lifestyle, etc.) and decides whether to issue
coverage and at what premium level.
2.Policy issuance
If approved, the insurer issues a policy detailing:
The death benefit amount.
Premiums and payment schedule.
Policy type (e.g., term or permanent).
Any riders (add‑ons).
California requires clear written disclosures, and for replacements (switching from one
life policy or annuity to another), specific forms and explanations must be provided
under the California Insurance Code and regulations.
[5] [7]
3.During the policy
You pay premiums as agreed.
In permanent policies (e.g., whole life), you may build cash value, subject to policy terms.
You may change beneficiaries or make other allowed changes.
4.At death
Beneficiaries file a claim with proof of death.
The insurer pays the death benefit if the claim is valid, subject to contestability rules
and policy terms.
CDI’s guides emphasize basic safeguards such as reading the entire policy, not signing blank or
incomplete forms, and ensuring the company is licensed in California.

Suitability and replacement rules
California law imposes specific rules when a life insurance policy or annuity is being replaced or
changed, to avoid harmful “churning” (unnecessary switching).Among other things:

Annuities in California: what they are
An annuity is a contract, usually issued by a life insurance company, in which you pay a lump
sum or series of payments and, in return, the insurer promises to make income payments to you
either immediately or in the future.While life insurance pays out upon death, annuities are
generally designed to pay while you are alive—often to provide income in retirement.
[8]
California’s consumer guides treat annuities as one of the primary tools for retirement income
planning, explaining that different types (fixed, variable, etc.) fit different needs and risk
tolerances.
[8] [7]
How annuities work in California
Mechanics are similar nationwide, but California overlays specific consumer‑protection and
suitability rules:
1.Accumulationphase
You pay premiums (either a single premium or multiple contributions).
The funds grow according to the contract type:fixed interest, index‑linked, or
market‑invested sub‑accounts (for variable annuities).
Surrender charges and tax rules apply if you withdraw early.
2.Payout phase
You can “annuitize” (convert the value into a stream of payments)or take systematic
withdrawals, depending on the contract.
Payment options include life‑only (pay as long as you live), joint‑and‑survivor, or period
certain (e.g., 10years).
3.Suited to long‑term needs
California emphasizes that annuities are long‑term products and that consumers
should carefully compare contracts, understand fees, and consider their time horizon
and liquidityneeds.
In California, when agents recommend annuities (especially to older consumers), they must
collect detailed information such as age, income, financial situation and needs, existing assets,
liquidityneeds, time horizon, risk tolerance, and tax status, in order to determine suitability.
This suitability information list is written into the California Insurance Code for annuity
recommendations.

California has detailed suitability standards for annuity sales, especially when an agent
recommends purchasing, exchanging, or replacing an annuity.
The recommendation must be based on reasonable grounds that the annuity is suitable
given the consumer’s disclosed financial situation, objectives, time horizon, liquidityneeds,
and risk tolerance.
The law lists specific “suitability information” that must be considered, including age,
annual income, financial situation and needs, existing assets (including life insurance and
investments), liquidityneeds, liquid net worth, risk tolerance, tax status, and whether the
consumer intends to apply for means‑tested government benefits.
These rules aim to ensure the consumer receives a tangible net benefit from the transaction
and reduce abusive sales practices.

Disclosure and buyer’s guides
Recent legislation such as SB 263 strengthens disclosure obligations around annuity sales in
California.Among other things, it requires that life insurers provide a buyer’s guide to all
consumers who purchase an annuity, helping them understand the product’s features, risks,
and alternatives.CDI’s consumer guides on annuities provide additional standardized

Licensing and training for annuity sellers
Agents who sell annuities must hold appropriate life insurance licenses issued by CDI.
[6] [8]
For variable annuities, agents must also have securities registration and provide a
prospectus describing investment options.
[8]
California has had a specific four‑hour annuity training requirement for agents, and SB 263
updates curriculum and training rules;CDIhas issued detailed annuity training curriculum
guidance.

Protections if the insurer fails
As with life insurance, owners of annuity contracts issued by companies licensed in California
may be partially protected by the California Life and Health Insurance Guarantee Association if
the insurer fails, subject to coverage limits.
